# AddH-out manuscript text draft

## Methods

The addH and addH-2 datasets were used as source-domain training data. Energy labels from the original `dat` and spreadsheet formats were standardized into a common table with unified sample identifiers, host oxide labels, dopant labels, and normalized H adsorption energies. AddH-out was treated as a target-domain validation set containing CeO2 and ZnO structures. Unless explicitly stated for few-shot calibration, AddH-out labels were not used for fitting source-domain models.

For each structure, we constructed a multimodal feature representation combining host and dopant descriptors, source-domain dopant statistics, local geometry descriptors around the adsorption site, graph embeddings extracted from pretrained FairChem/EquiformerV2 representations, and LLM-derived elemental priors. The LLM priors encoded chemically interpretable factors including dopant reducibility, oxygen affinity, charge-compensation complexity, host similarity, and expected H-binding rank. These features were used to train source-domain learners, including single-view and multiview regressors, graph-enhanced branches, residual/delta heads, and ensemble models selected using out-of-fold validation errors. A guarded superblend was used as the strict source-domain baseline.

To address the observed AddH-out domain shift, we introduced a chemistry-guided anchor model. This layer applied frozen host- and dopant-aware corrections motivated by oxide redox chemistry and H-binding trends, producing a physically constrained target-domain anchor before few-shot calibration. The final reported target-domain model used few-shot calibration on AddH-out: in each repeated split, a material-stratified subset of AddH-out labels was used only to estimate residual corrections relative to the chemistry-guided anchor, while the remaining AddH-out samples were held out for testing. We repeated this procedure across 500 material-stratified random splits and additionally evaluated leave-one-dopant-out splits. Performance was quantified using MAE, RMSE, Pearson correlation, and Spearman rank correlation. Calibration-fraction sensitivity was assessed by varying the fraction of AddH-out labels used for calibration from 10% to 80%.

## Results

The strict source-domain model showed substantial domain shift on AddH-out, with a held-out MAE of 1.667 +/- 0.350 eV and Spearman correlation of 0.546 +/- 0.176 across repeated material-stratified splits. Introducing the chemistry-guided anchor substantially improved both absolute accuracy and trend recovery, reducing the held-out MAE to 0.795 +/- 0.139 eV and increasing Spearman correlation to 0.886 +/- 0.057. After frozen few-shot target-domain calibration, the held-out MAE further decreased to 0.388 +/- 0.087 eV, with RMSE of 0.492 +/- 0.112 eV, Pearson correlation of 0.981 +/- 0.011, and Spearman correlation of 0.967 +/- 0.025. These results indicate that the main failure mode of the strict source-domain model is not random noise but systematic target-domain shift, which can be corrected by combining chemistry-guided anchoring with limited target-domain calibration.

Calibration-fraction sensitivity further supported this interpretation. With only 10% AddH-out labels used for calibration, the few-shot model remained close to the chemistry-guided anchor. At 20-30% calibration, the model began to consistently outperform the chemistry prior, reaching a mean held-out MAE of 0.498 eV and Spearman correlation of 0.958 at 30% calibration. At 50% or higher calibration fractions, the improvement became stable across repeated splits, with a 100% paired win rate over the chemistry-guided anchor at 50-70% calibration. Thus, the proposed workflow should be interpreted as an AddH-out few-shot domain-calibration strategy rather than a fully blind external prediction protocol.
